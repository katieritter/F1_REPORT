F1 Season Report Study Guide

Analytics Challenges

A concise guide to the data traps, interpretation caveats, and fixes used in the 2025 strategy and tyre report.

Source: knowledge_base/season-report-analytics-challenges.md
Challenge 01

Raw Lap Time Is Not Tyre Degradation

Cars usually get faster as fuel burns off, so raw lap time mixes tyre wear with fuel-load improvement.

  • Fuel burn can hide true tyre degradation.
  • Traffic, DRS trains, lift-and-coast, and safety cars add noise.
  • The report uses FuelCorrectedSeconds to isolate tyre wear better.
Remember: raw pace trends are not tyre-wear trends.
Challenge 02

Drying Tracks Break The Model

After rain, the track can improve faster than tyres degrade. That can create fake negative degradation.

  • The racing line dries and rubbers in.
  • Cars switch wet/intermediate/slick tyres.
  • Late laps may be fastest because the circuit improved, not because old tyres improved.
Australia, Britain, and Belgium are excluded from tyre analysis.
Challenge 03

Why Monza Can Show 0.000

A zero heatmap cell means no measurable positive slope, not literally zero physical tyre wear.

tiny tyre wear (+0.01 s/lap) + track evolution (-0.01 s/lap) ≈ flat fitted slope
  • Low-energy circuits have a very small tyre-wear signal.
  • Track rubbering-in can cancel that signal.
  • Negative fitted slopes are floored to zero as “no measurable degradation.”
Challenge 04

Cross-Race Comparisons Need Normalisation

Absolute lap times cannot be compared across circuits. A Monza lap and a Jeddah lap measure different baselines.

  • Quicklap filtering is applied per round.
  • Degradation is fitted within race/compound/team groups.
  • Season rankings aggregate slopes, not raw lap times.
Never use one global lap-time cutoff across a season.
Challenge 05

FastF1 Stint Count Overcounts Stops

Stint - 1 is unsafe because FastF1 can create phantom late-race stints.

  • Canada 2025 showed fake 4-6 stop races using the stint counter.
  • Tyre age kept increasing, proving those were not real tyre changes.
  • The report counts tyre changes directly.
real stop = TyreLife reset OR Compound change
Challenge 06

0-Stop Rows Are Usually DNFs

A car that retires early can appear as a 0-stop “strategy,” but that is not a strategic choice.

  • Stop-distribution charts require at least 90% race distance.
  • DNFs and very short races are excluded from strategy stop counts.
  • Stops of 4 or more are grouped as 4+.

Challenge 07

Pit Timing Uses OpenF1 stop_duration

The pit crew chart needs stationary time, not full pit-lane transit time.

  • Use stop_duration as stationary box time.
  • Do not use pit_duration; it is deprecated pit-lane duration.
  • Null stop_duration rows are excluded.
  • OpenF1 rate limits require throttling/retry logic.
Current dataset: 702 valid measured stops across all 24 races.
Challenge 08

Stop Count ≠ Stop Time

The report uses two separate pit concepts, and they answer different questions.

  • Stop count: how often tyres changed, from FastF1 lap/tyre data.
  • Stop time: how long the car was stationary, from OpenF1.
  • A team can stop often and execute quickly, or stop rarely but execute poorly.
Keep strategy behaviour separate from operational execution.
Practical Rules

The Report Pipeline Rules

1Use dry races only for tyre degradation and strategy-pattern charts.
2Use all races for pit-crew stationary timing.
3Never count stops from FastF1 Stint alone.
4Count tyre changes from TyreLife reset or Compound change.
5Exclude DNFs and short races from stop-distribution and aggression charts.
6Use OpenF1 stop_duration, not pit_duration, for pit-stop timing.
7Cap plotted pit-stop outliers for readability, but keep raw data unchanged.
8Treat chart narratives as plausible interpretations, not causal proof.
